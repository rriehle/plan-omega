## Executive Summary

Climate collapse represents a progressive, multi-decade threat that will fundamentally reshape habitability across the planet. For maritime families, the key advantages are **mobility**, **access to cooling ocean waters**, **fishing resources even as land agriculture fails**, and the **ability to relocate to climate refuges**. However, extreme weather intensification, ocean acidification, mass migration, and resource conflicts present severe challenges requiring strategic planning and adaptive response.

**Key Finding**: The window for gradual adaptation is closing rapidly. With current policies, we are tracking between RCP 4.5 and RCP 8.5 scenarios, not the RCP 2.6 pathway needed to limit warming to 1.5°C. Maritime families must plan for a 2-4°C warming scenario by 2100, with critical tipping points potentially occurring within the next 1-3 decades.

## 1. Climate Change Projections: Timeline and Severity

### Current State (2025)

- **Global Temperature**: Already ~1.2°C above pre-industrial levels
- **First Tipping Point Crossed**: Warm water coral reefs experiencing worst mass bleaching on record, with >80% affected since 2023
- **Ocean pH**: Decreased by 0.1 units since industrial revolution (30% increase in acidity)
- **Current Trajectory**: Somewhere between RCP 4.5 and RCP 8.5 scenarios

### Sea Level Rise Projections

**IPCC AR6 Findings** (relative to 1995-2014 baseline):

| Timeframe | Low Scenario (SSP1-1.9) | High Scenario (SSP5-8.5) |
|-----------|-------------------------|--------------------------|
| **2050** | 0.15-0.23 m (6-9 in) | 0.20-0.29 m (8-11 in) |
| **2100** | 0.28-0.55 m (11-22 in) | 0.63-1.01 m (25-40 in) |
| **2150** | 0.37-0.86 m (15-34 in) | 0.98-1.88 m (39-74 in) |

**Plausible Range by 2100**: 0.3-2.0 m (1-6.6 feet)

**Critical Implication**: Coastal cities, low-lying islands, and river deltas will experience catastrophic flooding. Maritime families can avoid these impacts through mobility.

### Extreme Weather Intensification

**Hurricane/Typhoon Changes**:

- **Current**: Observed increase in rapid intensification probability (1982-2017) across Atlantic, NW Pacific, globally
- **2019-2023**: 80% of Atlantic hurricanes intensified by average of 18 mph due to climate change
- **2°C Warming**: +13% of tropical cyclones reaching Category 4-5 strength
- **Category 6 Proposal**: Scientists propose new category for winds >192 mph
  - 5 historical storms (1980-2021) would qualify as Cat 6 - **all occurred in last 9 years**
  - 2°C scenario: Risk doubles in Gulf of Mexico, +50% near Philippines
- **Poleward Migration**: Storms reaching maximum intensity at higher latitudes (further north/south)
- **Season Extension**: Storms now occurring outside traditional June-November window; 5 of 161 named storms (2013-2023) occurred in May
- **Rainfall**: Projected increase due to enhanced atmospheric moisture

**Maritime Threat Assessment**: Intensifying storms with unpredictable timing require flexible seasonal migration strategies and enhanced weather monitoring. Traditional "hurricane season" boundaries becoming obsolete.

### Ocean Acidification and Fisheries

**Current State (2025)**:

- Surface ocean pH fallen by 0.1 units (30% acidity increase) since industrial revolution
- West Coast shellfish industry nearly collapsed in mid-2000s ($117M impact)
- Chinook/chum salmon hit record lows in 2021
- Coral reefs (first tipping point crossed): >80% affected by mass bleaching

**Projected Impacts**:

- **Food Web Disruption**: Species dependent on calcium carbonate shells (shellfish, pteropods, coral) face severe decline
- **Fisheries Catch**: Coral reef fisheries catch-per-unit-effort declined 60% since 1950, 30% since 2000
- **Biodiversity**: 63% of coral-reef-associated biodiversity has declined
- **Fish Migration**: Species moving northward/poleward as waters warm
  - Atlantic bluefin/yellowfin tuna: 4-10 km/year northward
  - Pacific cod, Alaska pollock shifting north
  - By 2030: 25% of global shared fish stocks will cross international boundaries
  - Tropics (Caribbean, Latin America, Oceania, South Asia) affected earliest

**Maritime Advantage**: While coastal fisheries collapse, pelagic (open ocean) fishing remains viable as species migrate. Mobile vessels can follow fish populations to productive waters.

### Climate Tipping Points: Status and Urgency

**Already Crossed**:

- ✅ **Coral Reefs**: Worst mass bleaching on record (2023-present), >80% affected

**High Risk (Near-Term)**:

- ⚠️ **AMOC Collapse** (Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation):
  - Could collapse this century, possibly before 2°C warming
  - Impacts: Europe deep freeze, tropics heating, disrupted monsoons, raised sea levels
  - Scientific debate: Not all experts agree collapse is imminent
- ⚠️ **Amazon Rainforest Dieback**:
  - Trigger threshold lowered from 2°C to 1.5°C
  - By 2050: Nearly half of Amazon could face multiple stressors triggering die-off
- ⚠️ **Ice Sheet Collapse**:
  - Sections may have already passed tipping points
  - Implies "several meters of irreversible sea level rise"

**Critical Implication**: These tipping points are interconnected and could cascade. AMOC collapse alone would fundamentally alter global weather patterns, affecting everything from fishing to safe sailing routes.

### Climate Refuges: Best Destinations (2030-2050)

**Top-Tier Refuges**:

3. **New Zealand**
   - **Advantages**: "Favorable starting conditions"; temperate climate; agricultural self-reliance; sea access; relatively isolated
   - **Maritime Access**: Excellent harbors; extensive coastline; mild climate
   - **Challenges**: Distance from other refuges; potential immigration restrictions

4. **Patagonia** (Argentina/Chile)
   - **Advantages**: Main Southern Hemisphere option; agriculture/settlement remain possible as temperatures rise
   - **Challenges**: Already suffering droughts; harsh weather conditions; limited infrastructure

**Secondary Refuges**:

   - **Immigration Potential**: Projected to attract millions fleeing coastal/western areas

6. **Southern Latitude Islands** (Falklands, South Georgia, Tasmania)
   - **Advantages**: Remote; temperate; ocean access
   - **Challenges**: Limited resources; harsh weather; isolation
   - **Best For**: Emergency refuge; experienced offshore sailors

**Regions to Monitor** (Mixed Outlook):

- **British Isles**: Temperate but vulnerable to AMOC collapse impacts
- **Pacific Northwest** (US/Canada): Good climate but wildfire risk, seismic risk
- **Alaska**: Warming rapidly; seasonal opportunities increasing but challenging conditions

### Strategic Geographic Implications for Maritime Families

**Seasonal Migration Pattern (Proposed)**:

- **Summer (Jun-Sep)**: High latitude refuges (Canada, Scandinavia, Great Lakes)
- **Winter (Dec-Mar)**: Mid-latitude zones avoiding tropics during intensifying cyclone season
- **Transition Seasons**: Flexible positioning based on weather patterns

**Critical Advantage**: A sailboat can relocate to follow habitable zones, avoid extreme weather, and access resources (fishing, freshwater) as they shift geographically.

### Maritime Survival Advantage: Freshwater Independence

**Rainwater Catchment**:

- Primary freshwater source for liveaboard vessels
- Climate change creates unpredictable precipitation (heavier downpours + longer droughts)
- **Critical**: Need large storage capacity (500+ gallons) to ride out extended droughts
- Example: Tuvalu residents rely almost entirely on rainwater, vulnerable to long droughts

**Desalination**:

- Provides drought-proof backup water source
- Not subject to precipitation changes or drought
- Used on seagoing ships, submarines globally
- Technology improving rapidly; costs declining
- **Recommendation**: Reverse osmosis watermaker as Alternate (P.A.C.E.) system

**Combined Strategy**:

- **Primary**: Rainwater catchment (free, sustainable, low energy)
- **Alternate**: Desalination (drought-proof, unlimited ocean supply)
- **Contingency**: Rationing + coastal resupply
- **Emergency**: Minimal consumption + emergency water tablets

**Critical Advantage**: While land-based populations fight over dwindling freshwater, maritime families have access to unlimited ocean water + desalination technology + rainwater. This is a **decisive survival advantage** in water scarcity scenarios.

## 7. Disease Range Expansion

**Projections**:

- **By 2050**: >1.3 billion new people exposed to Zika risk
- **By 2070**: +4.7 billion people at risk of malaria or dengue
- **By 2080**: 8 billion+ at risk; dengue impacts 60% of world population
- **Annual Transmission**: Malaria season +1 month; dengue season +4 months over next 50 years

**Geographic Expansion**:

- **Poleward Shift**: Tropical diseases spreading to temperate zones
- **Europe**: Malaria transmission predicted for 6 months/year in Southern/SE Europe (2051-2080); UK suitable for 2 months/year by 2030
  - 2022-2023: Malaria cases +62%, dengue/Zika/chikungunya +700%
- **Africa**: Highland regions (Ethiopia, Kenya, Rwanda) face increased malaria at higher elevations
- **Americas**: Latin America, Caribbean highlands newly vulnerable

**Temperature Dynamics**:

- Warming favors **dengue** (Aedes aegypti mosquito) expansion
- May reduce suitability for **malaria** (Anopheles gambiae) in some areas → disease burden shift

### Maritime Vulnerability

**High-Risk Activities**:

- Anchoring in tropical/subtropical coastal areas (mangroves, estuaries, lagoons)
- River navigation in endemic zones (Amazon, Mekong, West Africa, etc.)
- Landfalls in high-transmission areas for resupply

**Disease Prevention Strategy**:

1. **Primary**: Vector Control
   - Screened hatches, portholes, companionway
   - Mosquito netting over sleeping areas
   - Eliminate standing freshwater onboard (breeding sites)
   - Insect repellent (DEET, picaridin), permethrin-treated clothing
   - Anchor in breezy locations (mosquitoes weak flyers)

2. **Alternate**: Chemoprophylaxis
   - Antimalarials for high-risk zones (atovaquone-proguanil, doxycycline, mefloquine)
   - Consult travel medicine specialist; regional resistance patterns vary

3. **Contingency**: Rapid Diagnosis + Treatment
   - Carry malaria rapid diagnostic tests (RDTs)
   - Antibiotics for secondary infections
   - Artemisinin-based combination therapy (ACT) for malaria treatment
   - Medical evacuation plan (VHF, satellite phone, insurance)

4. **Emergency**: Supportive Care
   - Fever management, hydration, monitoring
   - Telemedicine consultation (if available)
   - Nearest medical facility identified pre-arrival

**Critical Implication**: Tropical/subtropical cruising grounds becoming more dangerous. High-latitude refuges (Scandinavia, Canada, Patagonia, New Zealand) have significant disease-avoidance advantage.

## 8. Emission Scenarios and Likely Trajectory

### Representative Concentration Pathways (RCPs)

**RCP 2.6** (Best Case - Aggressive Mitigation):

- Limits warming to <1.5-2°C by 2100
- Paris Agreement aspirational goal
- Requires immediate, drastic global emissions cuts
- **Current Assessment (2025)**: **Not on track** - only 1% probability

**RCP 4.5** (Moderate Mitigation):

- Stabilization scenario with some climate action
- ~2.4°C warming by 2100
- Significant but not catastrophic impacts

**RCP 8.5** (High Emissions - Worst Case):

- Business-as-usual; emissions rise through 21st century
- ~4.3°C warming by 2100
- Catastrophic global impacts
- **Current Assessment (2025)**: Less likely than when developed (2008-2011) due to renewable energy cost declines + Paris Agreement policies
- **BUT**: Only ~0.5% probability of exceeding 8.5 W/m² forcing by 2100

### Current Trajectory (2025)

**Consensus View**:

- We are tracking **between RCP 4.5 and RCP 8.5**
- Median projection: ~5.1 W/m² forcing by 2100 (range: 3.3-7.1)
- Without strengthened action, **unlikely to achieve RCP 2.6**
- Current policies insufficient to meet Paris Agreement targets

**Key Uncertainties**:

- Speed of renewable energy transition
- Tipping point cascades (could accelerate warming)
- Political will for sustained climate action
- Technological breakthroughs (carbon capture, fusion, etc.)

### Planning Implications for Maritime Families

**Conservative Planning Scenario**: **Assume RCP 6.0-7.0 trajectory** (between moderate and high)

- **2050**: ~2°C warming (high confidence)
- **2100**: 2.5-3.5°C warming (planning range)

**This Implies**:

- Sea level rise: 0.5-1.0 m by 2100
- Significantly intensified hurricanes
- Major agricultural zones uninhabitable
- Mass migration (100-200+ million people)
- Ocean acidification severely impacting fisheries
- Multiple tipping points likely crossed
- Resource conflicts widespread

**Optimistic Scenario** (RCP 4.5): Technology + policy breakthroughs limit warming to ~2.4°C

- Still severe impacts but manageable with adaptation

**Pessimistic Scenario** (RCP 8.5): Tipping point cascades + policy failures → >4°C warming

- Civilization-level collapse in many regions; only high-latitude refuges viable

**Critical Decision Point**: Maritime families should plan for **RCP 6.0-7.0 range** (prudent middle ground) while hoping for RCP 4.5 and preparing mentally for worst case.

## 9. Historical Analogues: Lessons from Past Climate Shifts

### Medieval Warm Period (c. 950-1250 CE)

**Characteristics**:

- Regional warming (not globally uniform)
- Temperatures ~0.5-1°C warmer in some regions (far less than current/projected warming)
- Peak warmth varied by region/timing

**Coastal/Maritime Impacts**:

- **Viking Greenland**: Warmed enough for farming, trade, exploration (1000-1300 CE)
  - ~620 farms established
  - Became impractical after 1300 as cooling began
- **Western Sardinia**: Coastal regions abandoned by Romans reoccupied; ports reestablished
- **Chesapeake Bay**: Large temperature excursions possibly linked to AMOC strength changes

**Lessons**:

- Climate shifts enabled **new maritime settlement zones**
- Warming created **temporary habitable regions** at high latitudes
- **Reversal** (Little Ice Age) was rapid and devastating for populations adapted to warmer conditions

### Little Ice Age (c. 1300-1850 CE)

**Characteristics**:

- Regional cooling (again, not globally uniform)
- Multiple cold periods; varied timing by location
- Particularly severe 1550-1850

**Coastal/Maritime Impacts**:

- **Atlantic Ice Expansion**: Blocked ports, disrupted fishing
- **Norse Greenland Collapse**: Colonies ended due to cooling, resource scarcity
- **European Ports**: Ice-choked harbors; shipping disrupted
- **Great Famine (1315-1317)**: Triggered by 3 years of torrential rains

**Lessons**:

- **Rapid Climate Shifts**: Can make previously viable regions uninhabitable within 1-2 generations
- **Fishing Disruption**: Ice, changed ocean currents impacted marine resources
- **Food Insecurity**: Climate-driven harvest failures → famine, social collapse
- **Population Decline**: Communities dependent on climate-sensitive resources (farming, fishing) abandoned regions

### Key Differences: Current Climate Change vs. Historical Analogues

| Factor | MWP/LIA | Current Climate Change |
|--------|---------|------------------------|
| **Speed** | Gradual (centuries) | Extremely rapid (decades) |
| **Magnitude** | ~0.5-1°C regional | 1.2°C already; 2-4°C projected |
| **Geographic Scope** | Regional variations | Globally coherent warming |
| **Sea Level Rise** | Minimal | 0.5-2.0 m by 2100 |
| **Ocean Acidification** | Not significant | Unprecedented in 300M years |
| **Human Population** | <500 million | 8 billion (16x larger) |
| **Technology** | Sail, subsistence farming | Diesel, industrial agriculture (vulnerable to collapse) |
| **Reversibility** | Natural cycles | Requires human intervention to stop |

### Implications for Maritime Survival

**Positive Lessons**:

- High-latitude regions can become productive (Greenland farming analogue)
- Maritime cultures can exploit new resources as climate shifts
- Mobility allows following resources (Vikings explored extensively)

**Cautionary Lessons**:

- Adaptation to new normal can be reversed rapidly (Norse Greenland collapse)
- Ice expansion could return (some models predict AMOC-collapse cooling in North Atlantic/Europe even as globe warms)
- Dependence on climate-stable food sources is risky
- Population pressures far greater now → less room for error

**Strategic Takeaway**: Historical analogues show climate shifts create **temporary opportunities** (new habitable zones, resources) but also **rapid reversals** (ice expansion, failed harvests). Maritime families must maintain **flexibility, redundancy, and self-sufficiency** rather than depending on any single climate-stable zone.

## 10. Maritime-Specific Survival Strategies

### Mobility: Core Strategic Advantage

**Why Mobility Matters**:

1. **Escape Extremes**: Leave hurricane zones, heat waves, flooded coasts, conflict areas
2. **Follow Resources**: Track fish migrations, freshwater, temperate climates
3. **Access Refuges**: Relocate to high-latitude safe zones as equatorial regions become uninhabitable
4. **Avoid Crowds**: Bypass overwhelmed regions attracting mass migration

**Seasonal Migration Strategy**:

| Season | Location Strategy | Primary Objectives |
|--------|-------------------|--------------------|
| **Summer (Jun-Sep)** | High latitudes (50-65°N/S) | Cool temperatures; long daylight; fishing; freshwater; avoid tropical cyclones |
| **Fall (Sep-Nov)** | Mid-latitudes (35-50°N/S) | Transition; harvest season; prepare for winter |
| **Winter (Dec-Mar)** | Temperate zones (25-40°N/S) | Avoid hurricane zones; stay outside tropics; mild climates |
| **Spring (Mar-May)** | Transition northward | Position for summer refuges; avoid early-season storms |

**Example Routes**:

- **Summer**: Scandinavia, Canada (Great Lakes, Maritimes), New Zealand (NZ summer = Dec-Feb)
- **Winter**: Portugal, Morocco (avoiding Sahara heat), Uruguay, Tasmania
- **Avoid**: Tropical/subtropical zones during intensifying cyclone seasons

**Critical Flexibility**: Traditional sailing routes (e.g., Caribbean winter, New England summer) may become obsolete. Climate-adaptive routing requires real-time weather data + multi-year planning.

### Ocean Temperature Moderation

**Advantage**:

- Ocean thermal inertia moderates temperature extremes
- Coastal areas cooler than inland during heat waves
- Offshore locations avoid urban heat island effects

**Strategy**:

- Anchor in bays, offshore islands during heat domes
- Use ocean water for cooling (heat exchangers, wet towels, swimming)
- Avoid inland navigation during extreme heat (rivers, canals can become dangerously hot)

**Limitation**:

- Ocean warming reduces this advantage over time
- Marine heat waves becoming more common
- Still significantly better than land-based exposure

### Fishing: Critical Food Source

**Strategy Overview**:

1. **Primary: Offshore Pelagic Fishing**
   - Target: Tuna, mahi-mahi, wahoo, marlin (open ocean species)
   - Methods: Trolling, handlines, small longlines
   - Advantage: Less impacted by coastal collapse, can follow migrations

2. **Alternate: Opportunistic Coastal Fishing**
   - Target: Whatever's available in transit (snappers, groupers, mackerel)
   - Methods: Bottom fishing, jigging, casting
   - Caution: Expect declining catches; don't depend on this

3. **Contingency: Aquaculture (if feasible)**
   - Concept: Towed nets with shellfish, seaweed cultivation
   - Challenges: Requires calm waters, extended stays; ocean acidification impacts shellfish
   - Opportunity: High-latitude refuges may have productive aquaculture zones

4. **Emergency: Foraging + Trade**
   - Coastal foraging (tide pools, beaches) for supplemental protein
   - Trade fish for vegetables, grains in port communities

**Critical Skills**:

- Learn fish identification, behavior, seasonal patterns
- Master multiple fishing techniques (trolling, jigging, bottom fishing, net fishing)
- Food preservation (salting, drying, smoking, canning)
- Navigate to productive fishing grounds using oceanographic data

### Freshwater Independence (P.A.C.E.)

**Primary: Rainwater Catchment**

- **System**: Large canvas/tarp catchment → filtration → storage tanks (500-1000+ gallons)
- **Advantages**: Free, sustainable, low energy, proven technology
- **Challenges**: Extended droughts (climate change increases variability); contamination risk; storage capacity limits
- **Best For**: Regions with reliable rainfall (high latitudes, temperate zones)

**Alternate: Desalination (Watermaker)**

- **System**: Reverse osmosis (12V/24V DC powered) or manual backup
- **Advantages**: Drought-proof, unlimited supply, predictable output
- **Challenges**: Energy demand (solar/wind required), maintenance, membrane life, cost
- **Best For**: Extended offshore passages; drought-prone regions; backup redundancy

**Contingency: Rationing + Coastal Resupply**

- Strict water discipline (Navy shower, dish washing minimization)
- Coastal springs, rivers (filter + purify)
- Port resupply when available

**Emergency: Minimal Consumption + Purification**

- 1 liter/day survival minimum
- Emergency water purification tablets (iodine, chlorine dioxide)
- Solar stills (last resort; very low output)

**Target Capacity**: 60-90 days autonomy at 2-3 gallons/day for family of 4 = **500-1000 gallon storage** + reliable production (rain catchment + watermaker).

### Hurricane/Storm Avoidance

**Traditional Strategy (Obsolete)**:

- Caribbean/tropics Nov-May (outside hurricane season)
- Temperate latitudes Jun-Oct (summer)

**Climate-Adapted Strategy**:

- **Assume Year-Round Tropical Cyclone Risk** (storms now occurring May, December)
- **Extend Avoidance Window**: Stay out of tropics Jun-Dec (possibly May-Jan)
- **Poleward Migration**: Expect storms at higher latitudes (35-40°N/S now vulnerable)
- **Real-Time Monitoring**: Satellite weather, SSB/HAM nets, GRIB files, tropical storm bulletins
- **Hurricane Holes**: Pre-identified safe anchorages (mangrove creeks, landlocked harbors, hurricane-rated marinas)
- **Inland Options**: Rivers, canals, lakes for storm refuge if coastal areas threatened

**Category 6 Preparedness**:

- Assume possibility of >192 mph winds in Gulf of Mexico, Caribbean, Philippines
- Traditional "safe" anchorages may be inadequate
- **Best Strategy**: **Don't be there** - avoid tropical cyclone zones entirely during peak season

### Energy Resilience

**Renewable Energy Priority**:

- **Solar**: Primary power source (panels + MPPT controllers + battery bank)
- **Wind**: Supplemental (wind generator for passage-making, high-latitude anchorages)
- **Hydro**: Towed generator for passage-making (if applicable)
- **Fossil Fuel Backup**: Diesel generator for emergencies, motor-sailing (but fuel supply chains vulnerable)

**Target**: 100% energy autonomy for lighting, navigation, communication, refrigeration, watermaker using renewables.

### Medical Preparedness: Disease + Injury

**Tropical Disease Regions**:

- Avoid or minimize time in expanding malaria/dengue zones
- Vector control (screens, netting, repellent, permethrin)
- Chemoprophylaxis for unavoidable high-risk areas
- Rapid diagnostic + treatment capability

**Isolation Advantage**:

- Distance from population centers reduces pandemic exposure
- Can quarantine at anchor during outbreaks
- Self-sufficiency reduces port visits (exposure points)

**Telemedicine**:

- Satellite internet for remote medical consultation
- Comprehensive medical library (digital + paper)
- Advanced first aid + minor surgery training

### Communication + Information

**Critical Systems**:

1. **VHF Radio**: Short-range; weather, port ops, distress
2. **SSB/HAM Radio**: Long-range; weather nets, maritime mobile service network, emergency
3. **Satellite Communication**: Email, weather GRIB files, internet (Starlink, Iridium)
4. **AIS**: Vessel tracking, collision avoidance
5. **EPIRB/PLB**: Distress beacon (satellite rescue alert)

**Information Needs**:

- Real-time weather (tropical storms, fronts, wind/waves)
- Geopolitical situation (conflicts, piracy, port closures)
- Climate refugee flows (avoid overwhelmed regions)
- Disease outbreaks (vector-borne, pandemic)
- Resource availability (fuel, food, water, parts in ports)

### Community + Networking

**Why It Matters**:

- Information sharing (safe harbors, weather, threats)
- Mutual aid (repairs, medical, defense)
- Trade/barter (skills, supplies, food)
- Social/psychological support (isolation, stress)

**Strategies**:

- Join cruising networks (SSB nets, online forums, regional rallies)
- Build relationships in refuge harbors (repeat visits, contribute to local communities)
- Ham radio networks (maritime mobile, emergency)
- Avoid complete isolation (humans are social species; mental health matters)

## 11. Scenario-Specific Recommendations

### Scenario A: Gradual Decline (RCP 4.5-6.0, 2025-2050)

**Timeline**: Progressive warming, increasing extremes, manageable adaptation

**Strategy**:

- **2025-2030**: Establish legal residency in climate refuge nation (Canada, Scandinavia, New Zealand)
- **2030s**: Transition to seasonal migration pattern (high-latitude summers, mid-latitude winters)
- **2040s**: Expect tropical/subtropical zones to become high-risk (heat, storms, water scarcity, migration, conflict)
- **2050+**: Operate primarily in high-latitude refuges (50-65°N/S) year-round as they become temperate

**Key Actions**:

- Build skills: Fishing, navigation, boat maintenance, medical, water systems
- Upgrade systems: Solar power, watermaker, communication, storm anchoring
- Develop refuge relationships: Port communities, local authorities, cruising networks
- Monitor tipping points: AMOC, ice sheets, Amazon (early warning for acceleration)

### Scenario B: Tipping Point Cascade (RCP 7.0-8.5, 2025-2050)

**Timeline**: AMOC collapses, Amazon dieback, ice sheets accelerate → abrupt, severe changes

**Strategy**:

- **Immediate (2025-2030)**: Relocate to high-latitude refuge; establish permanent base
- **2030s**: Expect chaos in collapse zones (Middle East, North Africa, South Asia, tropics)
  - Mass migration (200+ million)
  - Resource wars (water, food, fishing grounds)
  - Failed states (Somalia model spreading)
- **2040s-2050s**: Operate exclusively in stable refuge zones; avoid conflict regions entirely

**AMOC Collapse Implications**:

- **Europe**: Deep freeze (paradoxically, even as globe warms)
  - Scandinavia may become less viable; shift to Canada, New Zealand, Patagonia
- **North Atlantic**: Disrupted fishing, changed weather patterns, navigation hazards (ice expansion?)
- **Tropics**: Intensified heating, monsoon disruptions, agricultural collapse accelerates
- **Global**: Sea level rise accelerates (meters, not centimeters)

**Key Actions**:

- **Accelerate timeline**: Don't wait for 2030s to establish refuge base
- **Stock deep reserves**: 6-12 months food, critical spares, medical supplies
- **Hardening**: Prepare for isolation (limited resupply, ports closed, supply chains broken)
- **Defense**: Consider armed defense vs. piracy (regional laws permitting)
- **Mental preparation**: Civilization-level collapse is traumatic; psychological resilience critical

### Scenario C: Breakthrough Mitigation (RCP 3.0-4.5, 2025-2100)

**Timeline**: Technology + policy → aggressive emissions cuts; warming limited to ~2°C

**Strategy**:

- Traditional cruising grounds remain viable longer
- High-latitude refuges still advantageous (cooler, fewer extremes)
- Tropical zones challenging but not completely uninhabitable
- Focus on sustainable liveaboard lifestyle rather than survival mode

**Key Actions**:

- Continue skill-building, system upgrades (valuable regardless)
- Maintain flexibility (still expect increased storms, some displacement)
- Advocate for climate action (political engagement, community education)
- Enjoy cruising while it's still relatively stable

**Likelihood Assessment (2025)**: Low probability (~10-20%) based on current trajectory, but not impossible.

## 13. Summary and Strategic Recommendations

### Core Findings

1. **Climate collapse is underway**: First tipping point (coral reefs) crossed; AMOC, Amazon, ice sheets at high risk within decades
2. **Current trajectory**: Between RCP 4.5-8.5; 2-4°C warming by 2100 likely without dramatic policy shifts
3. **Timeline**: 2030s will see major disruptions (migration, conflicts, extremes intensify); 2050+ could be catastrophic in collapse zones
4. **Geographic divergence**: High latitudes (Canada, Scandinavia, New Zealand, Patagonia) become refuges; tropics/subtropics increasingly uninhabitable
5. **Maritime advantages**: Mobility, ocean cooling, fishing access, freshwater independence (rain + desalination), isolation from land-based collapse

### Strategic Priorities (P.A.C.E. Framework)

**Primary: Establish Refuge Base**

- Legal residency in high-latitude refuge nation (Canada, Scandinavia, New Zealand)
- Identify specific harbors, cruising grounds for seasonal rotation
- Build relationships with local communities, port authorities
- **Timeline**: 2025-2030 (before immigration restrictions tighten)

**Alternate: Seasonal Migration Pattern**

- Summer (Jun-Sep): High latitudes (50-65°N/S)
- Winter (Dec-Mar): Mid-latitudes (35-45°N/S), avoiding tropics
- Transition: Flexible based on real-time weather, geopolitics

**Contingency: Deep Self-Sufficiency**

- Water: Rain catchment + desalination (60-90 day autonomy)
- Food: Offshore fishing + preserved stores (6-12 months)
- Energy: Solar + wind (100% renewable autonomy)
- Medical: Advanced first aid, tropical disease prevention/treatment, telemedicine
- Communication: VHF + SSB/HAM + satellite (redundant long-range systems)

**Emergency: Isolated Survival**

- Assume supply chains broken, ports closed, no external support
- Vessel must be self-sufficient for 6-12 months
- Remote anchorages (avoid crowds, conflicts, piracy)
- Mental/psychological resilience for long-term isolation
