## Why These Skills Matter

Navigation and seamanship are the foundation of maritime survival. When electronic systems failwhether from EMP, power loss, or simple equipment failurethese skills become the difference between reaching safety and disaster. In collapse scenarios, GPS satellites may be destroyed, power grids will fail, and electronic charts become inaccessible. Your ability to navigate by sun, stars, and compass, and to handle your vessel in all conditions, determines your family's survival.

**Critical Scenarios**:

- GPS failure during ocean passage
- Electronic chart plotter loss in unfamiliar waters
- Storm approach requiring immediate heavy weather tactics
- Engine failure requiring expert sail handling
- Need to navigate to safe haven without external assistance
- Avoiding collision without radar or AIS

## Learning Progression

### Phase 1: Novice (0-3 months)

**Navigation Fundamentals**

- [ ] Read and plot courses on paper charts
- [ ] Calculate distance, speed, and time
- [ ] Use parallel rulers and dividers
- [ ] Take and plot compass bearings
- [ ] Understand chart symbols and depths
- [ ] Basic dead reckoning
- [ ] Tidal height calculations
- [ ] Simple current corrections

**Seamanship Basics**

- [ ] Points of sail and sail trim fundamentals
- [ ] Basic helm balance
- [ ] Simple anchoring (single anchor)
- [ ] Day shapes and navigation lights
- [ ] COLREGS (collision regulations)
- [ ] VHF radio procedures
- [ ] Man overboard prevention
- [ ] Safety equipment locations

### Phase 2: Intermediate (3-12 months)

**Advanced Navigation**

- [ ] Noon sun sights for latitude
- [ ] Compass deviation checks and corrections
- [ ] Running fixes and danger bearings
- [ ] Lead line depth sounding
- [ ] Estimated position with current and leeway
- [ ] Basic weather pattern recognition
- [ ] Barometric pressure interpretation
- [ ] Introduction to sight reduction

**Seamanship Development**

- [ ] Heaving-to in various conditions
- [ ] Storm sail deployment
- [ ] Multiple anchor techniques
- [ ] Night sailing procedures
- [ ] Reefing in increasing winds
- [ ] MOB recovery drills (Quick Stop method)
- [ ] Jury rig preparations
- [ ] Emergency steering methods

### Phase 3: Advanced (12+ months)

**Celestial Navigation Mastery**

- [ ] Morning and evening star sights
- [ ] Planet identification and sights
- [ ] Moon sights and corrections
- [ ] Complete sight reduction process
- [ ] Plotting multiple LOPs for fixes
- [ ] Lunar distance for time determination
- [ ] Latitude by Polaris (Northern Hemisphere)
- [ ] Emergency navigation without instruments

**Expert Seamanship**

- [ ] Heavy weather tactics selection
- [ ] Sea anchor and drogue deployment
- [ ] Storm strategy planning
- [ ] Lee shore escape tactics
- [ ] Damage control procedures
- [ ] Advanced jury rigging
- [ ] Kedging off groundings
- [ ] Emergency rudder construction

### Equipment Requirements

**Essential Navigation Tools**:

- Quality metal sextant ($700-2000) - Davis Mark 15 minimum, Astra IIIB preferred
- Backup plastic sextant ($150-250) - Davis Mark 3
- Current Nautical Almanac (annual)
- Sight Reduction Tables (HO 229 or HO 249)
- Plotting tools (parallel rulers, dividers, pencils)
- Hand-bearing compass
- Lead line with traditional markings
- Barometer (aneroid)
- Chronometer (GPS time on digital watch sufficient)

**Seamanship Equipment**:

- Storm sails (trysail and storm jib)
- Sea anchor (bow deployment)
- Drogue (stern deployment)
- Emergency tiller
- Spare halyards and sheets
- Bosun's chair
- Complete tool kit (see Chapter 14)

## Practice Drills

### Weekly Practice

- **Navigation**: Plot daily position by dead reckoning, even when GPS available
- **Seamanship**: Different sail combinations and trim adjustments
- **Safety**: Walk through emergency procedures

### Monthly Practice

- **Celestial**: Noon sun sight for latitude (compare with GPS)
- **Piloting**: Enter/exit harbor without electronics
- **Anchoring**: Set and retrieve in various conditions
- **MOB**: Quick Stop recovery drill (use fender as dummy)

### Quarterly Practice

- **Heavy Weather**: Heave-to in moderate conditions
- **Navigation**: Complete passage plan without electronics
- **Emergency**: Steering failure drill
- **Celestial**: Morning or evening star sights

### Annual Practice

- **Storm Preparation**: Full storm setup including sea anchor
- **Grounding**: Kedging off simulation
- **Damage Control**: Collision mat deployment
- **Offshore Passage**: Multi-day passage using only traditional navigation

## Proficiency Assessment

### Navigation Competency

You're ready when you can:

- [ ] Plot position within 5nm using noon sight alone
- [ ] Navigate 100nm passage without GPS and arrive within 10nm of destination
- [ ] Calculate set and drift, adjusting course accordingly
- [ ] Take and plot three-star fix in under 30 minutes
- [ ] Identify planets and navigation stars without reference
- [ ] Navigate in fog using sound signals and dead reckoning
- [ ] Determine compass deviation and create deviation table

### Seamanship Competency

You're ready when you can:

- [ ] Heave-to successfully in 30-knot winds
- [ ] Deploy storm sails in under 20 minutes
- [ ] Set Bahamian moor or two anchors in V-formation
- [ ] Recover MOB in under 5 minutes in moderate conditions
- [ ] Sail efficiently without engine for extended periods
- [ ] Reef or shake out reef in under 10 minutes
- [ ] Maintain control in 40+ knot winds and 15-foot seas

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Navigation Errors

1. **Forgetting variation and deviation** - Always apply compass corrections
2. **Ignoring current and leeway** - These accumulate significantly over time
3. **Poor sight timing** - Celestial bodies move; timing is critical
4. **Sextant handling** - Keep mirrors clean, check index error daily
5. **Over-reliance on single method** - Always use multiple position sources
6. **Inadequate chart scale** - Large-scale charts essential near shore
7. **Mixing units** - Confusion between nautical/statute miles, feet/fathoms/meters

### Seamanship Errors

1. **Delaying reefing** - "First thought of reefing = time to reef"
2. **Insufficient sea room** - Always maintain escape routes from lee shores
3. **Poor storm preparation timing** - Setup before conditions deteriorate
4. **Wrong heavy weather tactic** - Each boat behaves differently; know yours
5. **Inadequate anchor scope** - Minimum 5:1, prefer 7:1 in storms
6. **Ignoring chafe** - The #1 cause of gear failure in storms
7. **Crew fatigue** - Heaving-to for rest can save lives

## Advanced Topics

### Extreme Weather Navigation

- **Hurricane avoidance** - 1-2-3 rule and dangerous semicircle
- **Ice navigation** - Growler detection and ice-strengthened hull requirements
- **Fog navigation** - Sound signals, radar reflectors, extreme caution
- **Lightning protection** - Grounding systems and crew safety zones

### Emergency Navigation Techniques

- **Navigation by currents** - Using known currents when instruments fail
- **Soundings navigation** - Following depth contours
- **Bird and debris indicators** - Natural navigation signs
- **Improvised instruments** - Kamal, shadow boards, lead lines
- **Mental calculation methods** - Quick sight reduction approximations

### Professional Techniques

- **Great circle sailing** - Optimizing long passages
- **Weather routing** - Pilot charts and seasonal planning
- **Current exploitation** - Using ocean currents for speed
- **Composite sailing** - Combining great circle and rhumb line
- **Sight averaging** - Multiple sights for improved accuracy
